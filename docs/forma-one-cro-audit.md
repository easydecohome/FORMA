# FORMA ONE — CRO AUDIT & REWRITE

## 1 · CRITICAL PROBLEMS

Ranked by conversion damage:

1. **The footer ABN is "00 000 000 000".** A skeptic checking legitimacy finds a placeholder ABN — that's worse than no ABN. Same for "1300 000 000". One glance = "scam site" = gone forever. **Fix before any traffic. This is the single most dangerous element on the page.**
2. **Nobody is behind the business.** No name, no face, no "who we are" line. Anonymous + cheap + online-only is the exact pattern of every scam listing the buyer has been warned about. One founder line with a first name and a photo outperforms every design element on the page.
3. **The kitchen's dimensions are never stated.** The #1 practical question — "will it fit my wall?" — is unanswerable from the page. Skeptics don't message to ask; they leave.
4. **"Where is it made?" is not answered.** Every buyer of a $3,500 kitchen asks this in their head. Silence reads as hiding. Answer it confidently ("built in our partner factory to Australian spec, checked here") before they ask.
5. **No video.** For a no-showroom business, 60 seconds of shaky phone video is worth more than every photo. The slot exists in the code, empty.
6. **Freight is a black box.** "Quoted by postcode" is honest but pattern-matches to the classic gotcha. Indicative bands ("most east-coast metro: $X–$Y") defuse it.
7. **Voice drifts fancy in places.** "First deposits win," "reads premium," collections-speak inherited from the brand site. Cold traffic needs blunt words: what it is, what it costs, what's in the box, how to get one.
8. **Headline doesn't contain the price.** "A complete kitchen. One price. Delivered." forces a second look to learn the number. Cold traffic gives you one look.

What's already right (keep): real photos labelled as real, refundable-deposit risk reversal, scarcity with a stated reason, comparison table, assembly-catch honesty, sticky mobile CTA.

## 4 · PSYCHOLOGY BREAKDOWN

- **Price anchoring:** $15,000–$25,000 appears before $3,500 is justified. The buyer's brain does the discount, not our copy.
- **Reason-why scarcity:** "2 units" works because it's explained (small shipments, no warehouse). Unexplained scarcity reads as a countdown-timer trick; explained scarcity reads as a business model.
- **Commitment gradient:** the ask is never "$3,500" — it's "$200, refundable." Micro-commitment converts skeptics who would never wire four figures to a new site; the balance conversation happens after trust exists.
- **Named fear = disarmed fear:** "Buying a kitchen from a website you found today? We'd be skeptical too" — saying the buyer's exact thought out loud is the strongest trust move available to an unknown brand.
- **The catch, volunteered:** "you assemble it — that's the entire catch." Cheap + no stated catch = scam-pattern. Cheap + specific catch = business-model-pattern.
- **Concreteness = credibility:** 200,000-cycle hinges, mid-install photos, "$10,000+ you keep." Vague claims trigger skepticism; numbers and imperfect photos read as true.
- **Objections removed, in order:** Is this a scam? (founder, ABN, refundable) → Will it be rubbish? (hardware specs, real photos, video call) → What's the catch? (assembly, volunteered) → Will it fit? (dimensions) → What if it goes wrong? (replacement promise, warranty).
